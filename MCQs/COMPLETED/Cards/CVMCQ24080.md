### ü´Ä Pericardial Disease: Cardiac Tamponade Diagnosis

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE)** is the first-line test for suspected **cardiac tamponade**.
2. **Cardiac tamponade** occurs when fluid accumulation in the pericardial space compresses the heart and impedes **diastolic filling**.
3. When **intrapericardial pressure** rises enough to reduce **stroke volume** and **cardiac output**, tamponade may become **life threatening**.
4. Causes of **cardiac tamponade** include **malignancy**, **uremia**, and **inflammatory diseases** such as **systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)**.
5. Clinical signs of **cardiac tamponade** include **tachycardia**, **muffled heart sounds**, and **elevated central venous pressure with an absent y descent**.
6. **Pulsus paradoxus**, reflecting exaggerated **ventricular interdependence**, is a key clinical feature of tamponade.
7. **Electrocardiogram (ECG)** may show **low voltage** or **electrical alternans**, which are indicative of **pericardial effusion** but not necessarily tamponade.
8. Echocardiographic signs of tamponade include **early right ventricular diastolic collapse**, **late diastolic right atrial collapse**, **dilated noncollapsing inferior vena cava**, and **increased respiratory variation in mitral and tricuspid inflows**.
9. **Right heart catheterization** is rarely needed to diagnose tamponade unless there is concern for **constrictive pericarditis** or **effusive-constrictive pericarditis**.
10. **Coronary angiography** is not indicated in patients with **cardiac tamponade**.
11. **Computed tomography (CT)** scan should be avoided in **hemodynamically unstable** patients when tamponade is suspected.

1. TTE is readily available at the bedside and provides direct visualization of pericardial fluid and chamber collapse.
2. The hemodynamic effect occurs when pericardial pressure exceeds chamber pressure during diastole, limiting ventricular filling.
3. Reduced cardiac output in tamponade can result in hypotension and shock.
4. SLE is a known cause of pericardial effusion and was the underlying condition in the presented scenario.
5. These signs are part of **Beck's triad** and abnormal jugular venous waveform seen in tamponade.
6. Pulsus paradoxus refers to a drop in systolic blood pressure >10 mm Hg during inspiration.
7. Electrical alternans reflects swinging of the heart in a fluid-filled pericardium but is not diagnostic of tamponade by itself.
8. These findings are used to assess the degree of diastolic compression and impaired venous return.
9. Invasive hemodynamics are generally reserved for ambiguous cases or mixed pathology.
10. Coronary intervention is reserved for acute coronary syndromes, not pericardial compression syndromes.
11. Transporting unstable patients for CT delays diagnosis and may worsen outcomes.
