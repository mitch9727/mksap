### ü´† Pericardial Disease: Imaging Features of Constrictive Pericarditis

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Constrictive pericarditis** is characterized by **pericardial thickening**, **fibrosis**, and sometimes **calcification** that impair **diastolic filling** and limit **total cardiac volume**.  
2. **Chest radiography** or **computed tomography (CT)** may demonstrate **partial or circumferential pericardial calcification** in patients with **constrictive pericarditis**.  
3. **Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)** or **CT** may demonstrate **pericardial thickening greater than 3 mm** in constrictive pericarditis.  
4. The presence of **pericardial constriction** can exist **even in the absence** of **pericardial thickening** or **calcification**.  
5. On **chest radiograph**, **pericardial calcification** may be seen on both **posterior-anterior** and **lateral views**, as illustrated in the embedded image below:

### üìò Related Text Derivations

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Constrictive pericarditis** is most commonly idiopathic, but it can also be caused by **tuberculosis**, **cardiac surgery**, **chest irradiation**, **autoimmune disease**, or **bacterial infection**.
2. **Right-sided heart failure symptoms**, including **peripheral edema** and **abdominal swelling**, are the most common presentation of **constrictive pericarditis**.
3. **Exertional dyspnea** and **fatigue** in constrictive pericarditis are caused by **increased diastolic pressures** and an inability to increase **cardiac output** due to a **fixed stroke volume**.
4. Physical examination in constrictive pericarditis reveals **elevated central venous pressure** with **prominent x and y descents** in the jugular venous waveform.
5. **Kussmaul sign**, a paradoxical rise or lack of fall in jugular venous pressure on inspiration, is commonly seen in **constrictive pericarditis**.
6. **Pericardial knock** is an **early diastolic heart sound** in constrictive pericarditis, heard throughout the precordium, occurring earlier than **S‚ÇÉ**, and associated with prominent x and y descents.
7. **Peripheral edema**, **ascites**, **hepatomegaly**, and **pleural effusions** are common findings in **constrictive pericarditis**.
8. **Electrocardiogram (ECG)** may show **low voltage** in constrictive pericarditis, but no ECG finding is specific.
9. **Transthoracic echocardiography** in constrictive pericarditis shows **normal ventricular size and systolic function**, but **dilated inferior vena cava** due to elevated right atrial pressure.
10. **Tissue Doppler** and **Doppler echocardiography** help differentiate **constrictive pericarditis** from **restrictive cardiomyopathy**, based on the presence of **preserved myocardial relaxation**.
11. **Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)** may show **inspiratory septal shift**, consistent with **ventricular interdependence**.
12. **Right heart catheterization** in constrictive pericarditis demonstrates a **dip-and-plateau** (square root) sign and **ventricular pressure discordance** during respiration.
13. During inspiration, **right ventricular systolic pressure increases** while **left ventricular systolic pressure decreases** in constrictive pericarditis, demonstrating **ventricular interdependence**.
14. **Pulsus paradoxus** is less commonly seen in constrictive pericarditis compared with **cardiac tamponade**.
15. In **transient constrictive pericarditis**, symptoms may include **fever**, **chest pain**, and **elevated inflammatory markers** such as **erythrocyte sedimentation rate** or **C-reactive protein**.
16. **Pericardial effusion** is more likely in patients with **transient constrictive pericarditis** than in those with fixed constriction.
17. **Pericardiectomy** is effective in **constrictive pericarditis**, but not in **restrictive cardiomyopathy**, making distinction between the two conditions essential.
