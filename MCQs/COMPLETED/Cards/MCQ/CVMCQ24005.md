### ü´† Aortic Disease: Acute Ascending Aortic Dissection

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Acute aortic dissection** is a potential mimic of **ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI)** and can present with ST-segment elevation if the dissection involves the **right coronary artery ostium**, resulting in **myocardial ischemia**.
2. **Diagnostic clues** to acute ascending aortic dissection include **tearing chest pain radiating to the back**, **differential upper extremity pulses or blood pressures**, **aortic insufficiency**, **pulsus paradoxus**, and **mediastinal widening on chest radiograph**.
3. **Computed tomography angiography (CTA)**, **magnetic resonance angiography**, and **transesophageal echocardiography (TEE)** have **similar sensitivity and specificity** for diagnosing **acute thoracic aortic disease**.
4. **Computed tomography angiography (CTA)** is the most commonly performed imaging modality in suspected **aortic dissection** due to its **speed** and **widespread availability**.
5. **Acute thoracic aortic dissection** is a **surgical emergency** that requires **emergent thoracic surgery**.
6. In cases of suspected **acute aortic dissection**, **primary percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI)** is inappropriate and may delay necessary surgical treatment due to the need for **anticoagulation** and **dual antiplatelet therapy**.
7. In **acute aortic dissection**, **intravenous fluid resuscitation** is not appropriate in the setting of **aortic insufficiency** and **pulmonary edema**.
8. In **acute ascending aortic dissection**, neither **intravenous anticoagulation** (e.g., **unfractionated heparin**) nor **thrombolytic therapy** is appropriate due to increased **bleeding risk** and interference with pending **surgical intervention**.
9. The only initial medical therapy with benefit in **acute ascending aortic dissection** is **blood pressure control**, usually achieved with **intravenous Œ≤-blockers**.

#### üí¨ Extra
1. ST-segment elevation in the inferior leads may occur if the dissection compromises coronary perfusion.
3. Despite equivalent diagnostic accuracy, CTA is preferred in most emergency settings.
4. CTA is readily accessible in most emergency departments and provides rapid imaging of the entire thoracic aorta.
5. Surgical intervention often involves emergent repair of the ascending aorta to prevent rupture or tamponade.
6. Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor) and anticoagulation complicate intraoperative management due to bleeding risk.
7. Volume overload may worsen left-sided filling pressures and pulmonary edema in patients with aortic regurgitation.
8. These therapies are commonly used in myocardial infarction but are contraindicated in dissection.
9. Œ≤-blockers reduce shear stress on the aortic wall by lowering heart rate and blood pressure.
