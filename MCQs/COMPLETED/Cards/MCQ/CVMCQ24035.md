1. Patients with **congenital heart disease** are at increased risk for **infective endocarditis**, especially those with **unrepaired defects**, **repaired disease with residual defects**, or **prosthetic material** such as a **patch** or **valve**.
2. **Infective endocarditis** may present with **nonspecific symptoms**, including **fever**, **fatigue**, and **malaise**, particularly in patients with underlying **congenital heart disease**.
3. **Right-sided infective endocarditis** may follow an **indolent course** and may lack typical systemic findings such as high fever or leukocytosis.
4. A **high index of suspicion** for **infective endocarditis** is warranted in any patient with a history of **congenital heart disease** who presents with symptoms potentially attributable to endocarditis.
5. At least **two sets of blood cultures** should be obtained when even a modest probability of **infective endocarditis** exists.
6. **Echocardiography** should be performed when **infective endocarditis** is suspected in patients with **congenital heart disease**.
7. In patients with **prosthetic valve endocarditis**, **vancomycin and cefepime** are an appropriate empiric antibiotic regimen.
8. **Antibiotic therapy** should be initiated as soon as possible in **infective endocarditis**, but only **after blood cultures are obtained**, unless the patient is hemodynamically unstable or in septic shock.
9. **C-reactive protein** and **procalcitonin** may be elevated in **infective endocarditis**, but they are **not reliable** for assessing the likelihood of disease.
10. **Clinical observation alone** is not appropriate for patients at high risk for **infective endocarditis**, even in the absence of fever or leukocytosis.

1. The presence of a **bioprosthetic valve** and **poor dentition** further increases endocarditis risk.
