### ü´† Cardiology: Coronary Artery Disease After Chest Irradiation

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Thoracic irradiation therapy** in childhood may cause long-term damage to the **pericardium**, **myocardium**, **valves**, **coronary vasculature**, and **conduction system**, typically manifesting clinically two to three decades after treatment.
2. **Coronary artery disease (CAD)** caused by **radiation therapy** often involves **ostial**, **long**, **smooth**, and **concentric lesions** with higher **fibrotic content** than standard atherosclerotic lesions.
3. Traditional cardiovascular risk factors such as **smoking**, **dyslipidemia**, and **hypertension** increase the incidence of **radiation-induced CAD**, and aggressive management of these risk factors is indicated.
4. Young adults who received **chest irradiation** during childhood and present with **chest pain** should undergo prompt evaluation for **coronary artery disease (CAD)**.
5. **Regadenoson myocardial perfusion imaging** is an appropriate diagnostic modality for evaluating **myocardial ischemia** in patients with suspected **radiation-induced CAD**.
6. **Conduction system abnormalities** such as **ECG conduction delays** may be a manifestation of prior **radiation-induced cardiac damage**.
7. In the absence of **pleuritic chest pain**, **friction rub**, or **typical ECG changes**, **acute pericarditis** is less likely despite a history of thoracic irradiation.
8. In younger patients with chest pain and no clear cause, **gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)** may be considered, but **radiation-induced CAD** must be prioritized in those with a history of thoracic irradiation.
9. **Transthoracic echocardiography** is useful for evaluating **valvular** and **pericardial disease**, but **stress testing** is preferred when **coronary artery disease** is the main concern.
