### ü´† Pericardial Disease: Acute Pericarditis Diagnosis

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Acute pericarditis** is the most common **inflammatory cardiac disorder** and is often **idiopathic**, frequently following a **recent viral infection**.
2. Common causes of **acute pericarditis** include **viral infections** (e.g., enterovirus, adenovirus, parvovirus), **autoimmune disorders** (e.g., systemic lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis), **cardiac surgery**, and **uremia**.
3. The diagnosis of **acute pericarditis** requires at least **two of the following criteria**: **pleuritic chest pain**, **pericardial friction rub**, **electrocardiogram (ECG) changes** including **ST-segment elevation in a noncoronary distribution with PR-segment depression**, and **pericardial effusion**.
4. **Acute coronary syndrome (ACS)** typically causes **ST-segment elevation** in a **coronary distribution** and is associated with **elevated cardiac biomarkers** such as **troponin**.
5. The presence of **ST-segment elevation in a noncoronary distribution** with **PR-segment depression** on ECG suggests **acute pericarditis** rather than **ACS**.
6. **Acute myopericarditis** involves **myocardial inflammation**, typically evidenced by **elevated cardiac biomarkers** (e.g., troponin, N-terminal pro‚ÄìB-type natriuretic peptide) or **abnormal cardiac imaging**.
7. A **normal troponin level** makes the diagnosis of **myocardial involvement** in **acute myopericarditis** unlikely.
8. **Acute pulmonary embolism** may present with **pleuritic chest pain** and **nonspecific ST-T‚Äìwave abnormalities** or **incomplete right bundle branch block** on ECG.
9. A **normal D-dimer level** makes **acute pulmonary embolism** unlikely in a low-risk patient.

#### üí¨ Extra
1. Most idiopathic pericarditis cases are presumed postviral even when no organism is isolated.
3. Diagnostic criteria help differentiate pericarditis from other causes of chest pain and ECG changes.
4. The duration of chest pain in ACS is typically shorter, and cardiac biomarkers rise within hours.
6. Myopericarditis may require **cardiac magnetic resonance imaging** or **echocardiography** to detect wall motion abnormalities.
8. ECG findings in pulmonary embolism are usually nonspecific and less diagnostic than those of pericarditis.
9. D-dimer is most useful for ruling out pulmonary embolism in low-pretest-probability patients.

13. **Cardiac computed tomography (CT)** may also demonstrate **pericardial inflammation** in **acute pericarditis**.
14. Additional signs supporting **acute pericarditis** include **fever**, **leukocytosis**, and elevated **erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)** or **C-reactive protein (CRP)**.
