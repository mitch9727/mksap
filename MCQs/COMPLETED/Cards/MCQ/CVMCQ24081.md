1. **Cardiac tamponade** with hemodynamic compromise requires **immediate pericardial drainage**.
2. **Pericardiocentesis** is the preferred initial method for pericardial drainage and can be performed via a **subxiphoid approach** guided by **echocardiography** or **fluoroscopy**.
3. Clinical signs of **cardiac tamponade** include **tachycardia**, **pulsus paradoxus**, **elevated central venous pressure** with **absent y descent** of the jugular venous pulse, and **muffled heart sounds**.
4. **Systolic blood pressure** may be normal early in tamponade due to the **adrenergic response**, but eventually declines as **intrapericardial pressure increases**.
5. **Echocardiography** is the best initial test for tamponade and may reveal **early diastolic right ventricular collapse**, **late diastolic right atrial collapse**, and **abnormal interventricular septal motion**.
6. **Emergent pericardial window surgery** is indicated if **pericardiocentesis is not feasible** or if **fluid reaccumulates** after initial drainage.
7. **Prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC)** or **andexanet alfa** may reverse anticoagulation but do **not replace the need for emergent pericardial drainage** in tamponade.
8. In the setting of tamponade, **device extraction** is not the immediate priority, even if the tamponade is presumed due to **pacemaker lead perforation**.
