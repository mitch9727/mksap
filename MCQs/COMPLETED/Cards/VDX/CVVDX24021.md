#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Ventricular tachycardia (VT)** is characterized by **regular wide QRS complexes**, a **pulse rate >120/min**, and **atrioventricular (AV) dissociation** (often demonstrated by **capture** and/or **fusion beats**).
2. **VT** may be classified as **sustained** (lasting >30 seconds) or **nonsustained**, and as **monomorphic** (same QRS morphology throughout) or **polymorphic** (variable QRS morphology).
3. The development of **VT** is facilitated by **abnormal ventricular conduction** and/or **myocardial scar tissue**.
4. **VT** usually occurs in patients with **structural heart disease**, including **ischemic** and **nonischemic cardiomyopathy**.
5. On ECG, a **fusion beat**, **abrupt axis change**, and **QRS widening** can mark the onset of **VT**.

#### üí¨ Extra
1. AV dissociation confirms the ventricular origin of the rhythm; fusion and capture beats are hallmark features.
2. **Torsades de pointes** is an example of polymorphic VT that twists around the baseline.
3. Myocardial scar tissue creates a substrate for reentrant circuits, a common mechanism for VT.
4. Conditions like prior myocardial infarction or dilated cardiomyopathy are typical structural substrates.
5. The ECG showed transition to VT one third through the tracing, marked by a fusion beat.

10. **Differential diagnosis** for wide-complex tachycardia includes **supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) with aberrant conduction**, **preexcited SVT (antidromic)**, and **ventricular paced rhythms**.
