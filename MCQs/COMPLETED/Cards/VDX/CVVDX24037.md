1. **Cardiac implantable electronic device (CIED) infections** can present as **pocket infections**, **endocarditis**, or both.
2. Most **CIED infections** are caused by **Staphylococcus epidermidis** and **Staphylococcus aureus**.
3. Symptoms of **CIED infection** may include **fever**, **chills**, **malaise**, **lassitude**, and **failure to thrive**.
4. **CIED infections** often have an **insidious onset and presentation**.
5. Physical examination findings of **CIED pocket infection** may include **erythema**, **swelling**, **drainage**, **wound dehiscence**, or **device erosion**.
6. **Up to 5%** of patients with a **CIED** will develop an infection within **3 years** of implantation.
7. **CIED infections** may involve the **generator pocket**, the **leads**, or the **entire device**.
8. Diagnosis of **CIED infection** requires a **high index of suspicion** due to potentially **subtle presentation**.
9. Patients with suspected **CIED infection** should undergo **urgent specialist evaluation**, **blood culture testing**, and **echocardiography**.
10. **Empiric antibiotics** should not be initiated **before obtaining blood cultures** because they may obscure the diagnosis.
11. **Diagnostic aspiration** of the CIED pocket is **contraindicated** due to the risk of introducing infection.
12. Effective treatment of **CIED infection** includes **complete device removal**, **pocket debridement**, **sustained antibiotic therapy**, and **reimplantation at a new site** after infection clearance.
13. Patients with **pocket-only CIED infections** typically have **negative blood cultures** and **no echocardiographic evidence** of lead or valve vegetation.
14. Management of **pocket-only CIED infections** includes **removal of the entire device** and **2 weeks of antibiotics**.
15. Patients with **more extensive CIED infections** require **4 to 6 weeks of antibiotics** and **device removal** if they meet any of the following criteria:
