### ü´Ä Pericardial Disease: Pericardial Friction Rub

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. A **pericardial friction rub** is the predominant auscultatory finding in **acute pericarditis**.
2. **Pericardial friction rubs** are typically **triphasic**, with components corresponding to **atrial systole**, **ventricular systole**, and **ventricular filling**, but may also be **biphasic** or **monophasic**.
3. **Pericardial rubs** are often described as **creaking**, **grating**, or **scratching** sounds.
4. The rub is best heard at the **left sternal border** with the patient **sitting up**, **leaning forward**, and **holding breath in end-expiration**.
5. A **pericardial rub** may vary in intensity with **respiration** but is **not directly affected** by it.
6. The presence of a **pericardial friction rub** is one of the **four diagnostic criteria** for **acute pericarditis**.
7. **Pleural friction rubs** differ from **pericardial rubs** by having **two components** related to **respiration** rather than three cardiac cycle phases.

#### üí¨ Extra
2. The triphasic nature of the pericardial rub helps distinguish it from other types of friction rubs and murmurs.
6. The other three criteria for diagnosing acute pericarditis are: typical **chest pain**, **new ECG changes**, and **new pericardial effusion**.
7. This distinction is clinically important during auscultation to avoid misdiagnosis.

### üìò Related Text Derivations

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Acute pericarditis** is clinically diagnosed by the presence of at least **two of four criteria**: **typical chest pain**, **pericardial friction rub**, **new ECG changes**, or **new pericardial effusion**.
2. **Chest pain** in acute pericarditis is typically **sharp**, **severe**, **positional**, and **worsens when supine**, improving when **sitting up and leaning forward**.
3. The **typical ECG pattern** in acute pericarditis includes **concave ST-segment elevation** in multiple leads and possibly **PR-segment depression** in lead II or **reciprocal PR elevation** in lead aVR.
4. The **absence of a pericardial effusion** does **not exclude** a diagnosis of **acute pericarditis**.
5. **Echocardiography** should be performed to evaluate for **pericardial effusion**, and **cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (CMR)** or **gated cardiac computed tomography (CT)** may show evidence of **pericardial inflammation**.
6. Additional findings that support the diagnosis of acute pericarditis include **fever**, **leukocytosis**, and elevated **erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)** or **C-reactive protein (CRP)**.
7. **Troponin levels** may be **normal or mildly elevated** in **myopericarditis**.
8. **Tuberculosis** should be considered as a cause of pericarditis in **at-risk populations**, including those from **endemic regions** or residing in **chronic care facilities**.
9. **Peri-infarct pericarditis (PIP)** occurs **1 to 2 days after myocardial infarction**, is more common with **fibrinolytic therapy**, and is usually **self-limited**.
10. **Postpericardiotomy syndrome** is a **delayed autoimmune** form of pericarditis occurring **weeks after cardiac surgery**, with a clinical presentation similar to **idiopathic pericarditis**.

#### üí¨ Extra
1. The four diagnostic criteria are central to confirming acute pericarditis in the absence of a gold-standard test.
3. These ECG changes help differentiate pericarditis from **acute myocardial infarction**, which follows a single coronary distribution.
5. **Late gadolinium enhancement** on CMR is a key marker of **active pericardial inflammation**.
8. Risk factors for tuberculous pericarditis include healthcare exposure and endemic travel.
9. **PIP** is distinguished from **Dressler syndrome** by its **timing** and **etiology**.
10. Management for postpericardiotomy syndrome is similar to that of **idiopathic or viral pericarditis**.
