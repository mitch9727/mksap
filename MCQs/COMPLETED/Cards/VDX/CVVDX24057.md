### üìò Related Text Derivations

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Atrial flutter** is an **organized macro-reentrant tachycardia** with **discrete regular atrial activity** on electrocardiogram (ECG).
2. The typical **atrial rate in atrial flutter** ranges from **250 to 300 beats per minute**.
3. **Typical atrial flutter** is most commonly due to **counterclockwise reentry around the tricuspid annulus** and occurs in patients **without prior cardiac disease**.
4. **Typical atrial flutter** is characterized by a **sawtooth pattern** with **inverted flutter waves in leads II, III, and aVF**, and **positive flutter waves in lead V1**.
5. **Atypical atrial flutter** is more common in patients with **prior atrial fibrillation ablation** or **cardiac surgery**, with **reentry circuits in alternate atrial locations**.
6. **Anticoagulation management** for chronic **atrial flutter** follows the same principles as for **atrial fibrillation**.
7. **Rate control in atrial flutter** is often difficult and may require **multiple high-dose AV nodal blocking agents**.
8. **Catheter ablation** is the **definitive treatment for typical atrial flutter**, with a **success rate >95%** and **low complication risk**.

#### üí¨ Extra
1. Macro-reentry circuits are the hallmark of atrial flutter, distinguishing it from focal atrial tachycardia.
2. The rapid but regular atrial rate helps differentiate flutter from fibrillation.
3. Counterclockwise reentry causes negative flutter waves in inferior leads.
4. These electrocardiographic features help confirm the diagnosis of typical flutter.
5. Surgical or ablation scars alter atrial conduction pathways, promoting atypical flutter.
6. Stroke prevention strategies are shared with atrial fibrillation due to similar embolic risk.
7. AV node blockers like **beta blockers**, **calcium channel blockers**, or **digoxin** may be insufficient alone.
8. Ablation targets the isthmus-dependent flutter circuit between the tricuspid annulus and inferior vena cava.
