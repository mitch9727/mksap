1. **Acute epiglottitis** is a severe inflammatory condition that can cause partial or complete **upper airway obstruction**.  
2. Findings suggesting **partial airway obstruction** include tachypnea, stridor, increased respiratory effort, and an upright (tripod) posture with use of accessory muscles.  
3. Inspiratory stridor suggests **obstruction at or above the vocal cords**, whereas expiratory stridor suggests an **intrathoracic process**.  
4. High-grade airway obstruction should be suspected with absent air movement, inability to talk, or cyanosis.  
5. Pulse oximetry in partial upper airway obstruction is often normal, and oxygen desaturation is a late or preterminal finding.  
6. In patients with respiratory distress or high risk for respiratory deterioration, **early intubation with a cuffed endotracheal tube** is indicated.  
7. Endotracheal intubation for acute upper airway obstruction should be performed early and in a controlled setting by clinicians with expertise in **difficult airway management** and emergent tracheostomy.  
8. Noninvasive positive-pressure ventilation has no established role in treating **upper airway obstruction** outside of obstructive sleep apnea.  
9. A helium-oxygen mixture may reduce the work of breathing in severe airway obstruction, but it is not supported for use in epiglottitis.  
10. The use of **racemic epinephrine** in acute epiglottitis is controversial and not supported by evidence.  
11. **Key Point:** Endotracheal intubation should be performed early and in a controlled setting in critically ill patients with worsening upper airway obstruction.
