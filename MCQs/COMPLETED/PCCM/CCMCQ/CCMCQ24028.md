1. Brain death occurs when **no cerebral or brainstem function is evident**, most commonly from anoxic injury, trauma, or intracranial hemorrhage.  
2. The diagnosis of brain death requires: an underlying cause known to cause cerebral insult, coma, absence of cranial nerve reflexes, absence of motor reflexes involving the brain, and lack of respiratory drive.  
3. Confounding factors such as hypothermia, hypotension, severe electrolyte or acid-base disturbances, toxins, or sedative medications must be absent before evaluation for brain death.  
4. In patients with a core body temperature <36.0 Â°C (96.8 Â°F), rewarming to â‰¥36.0 Â°C and a waiting period of at least 24 hours are required before brain death evaluation.  
5. A detailed neurologic examination with testing of brainstem reflexes is essential, and performing two independent examinations may reduce diagnostic error.  
6. Some spinal reflexes, including finger flexor movements, plantar flexion, myoclonus, and muscle fasciculation, may persist despite brain death.  
7. An **apnea test** (removal of ventilator support) demonstrating absent respiratory drive despite hypercapnia is a **necessary component** of the diagnosis of brain death.  
8. Ancillary tests, such as radionuclide perfusion brain imaging, are used only if there is uncertainty in the diagnosis or if the apnea test cannot be performed (eg, chronic hypercapnia).  
9. Electroencephalography (EEG) is not appropriate for diagnosing brain death because it evaluates only cerebral hemisphere activity and not brainstem function.  
10. U.S. state- and institution-specific criteria may need to be satisfied in addition to clinical and physiologic testing before a patient can be declared brain dead.
