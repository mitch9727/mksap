
### ü´Å Critical Care Medicine: ICU Delirium Management

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Delirium** is an acute change in cognitive functioning, characterized by **impaired attention** and **disorientation** with **fluctuating mental status**.
2. **Intensive care unit (ICU) delirium** is extremely common and is associated with **increased ICU length of stay**, **mortality**, and **post‚ÄëICU cognitive impairment**.
3. Risk factors for **ICU delirium** include **benzodiazepine use**, **polypharmacy**, **preexisting cognitive impairment**, **older age**, **pre‚ÄëICU surgery or trauma**, and **high severity of illness on ICU admission**.
4. **Early identification and correction of underlying causes** are critical in the management of ICU delirium.
5. **Multicomponent interventions**, such as the **ABCDEF bundle**, decrease delirium duration, reduce ICU length of stay, and improve survival.
6. **Early mobilization** with physical therapy is an effective component of ICU delirium management.
7. For patients requiring ICU sedation, **dexmedetomidine** or **propofol** is preferred; however, dexmedetomidine does **not** treat delirium itself.
8. **Antipsychotics** (for example, **haloperidol** and atypical antipsychotics) have **not** been shown to prevent or treat delirium and may cause **harm**; they should be **avoided** unless severe refractory agitation prevents essential care.
9. **Neuroimaging** (for example, **head CT**) is **not routinely required** in ICU delirium unless a **structural cause** is suspected.

2. ICU delirium contributes to acute complications (for example, prolonged ventilation) and chronic complications (for example, long‚Äëterm cognitive impairment).

8. Pharmacologic sedation may be required only for severe agitation that threatens safety or prevents essential care.

1. **Predisposing factors** for delirium include **advanced age (‚â•70 years)**, **sensory impairment**, **preexisting cognitive impairment**, **poor nutritional/functional status**, **multiple comorbidities**, **kidney disease**, **alcohol use disorder**, **depression**, and **polypharmacy**.
2. **Provoking factors** for delirium include **deliriogenic medications** (for example, **benzodiazepines**, **anticholinergics**, **opioids**), **sleep deprivation**, **constipation**, **dehydration**, **urinary retention**, **severe illness**, **infection**, **hypoxia**, **surgical stress**, **tethers** (for example, **urinary catheter**), and **sensory deprivation**.
3. **Clinical features** of delirium include **fluctuating mentation**, **hyperactive or hypoactive states**, **disorganized thoughts**, **delusions**, **hallucinations**, **poor attention**, **altered circadian rhythm**, **tremors** (with **myoclonus**), **hyperreflexia**, **restlessness or somnolence**, **amnesia**, and **increased sympathetic tone** (particularly with **alcohol withdrawal**).
4. **Nonpharmacologic management** of delirium includes **family engagement**, **daytime light exposure**, **calm redirection**, **pain control**, **early mobility and exercise**, **avoiding restraints**, and **repeat neurologic examinations**.
5. **Physiologic optimization** in delirium includes **maintaining oxygen saturation above 92%** and, in the ICU, performing **spontaneous awakening trials (SATs)** and **spontaneous breathing trials (SBTs)**.
