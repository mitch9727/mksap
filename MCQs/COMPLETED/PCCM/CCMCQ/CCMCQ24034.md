### ü´Å Critical Care Medicine: Nutritional Support in Critically Ill Patients

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Malnutrition** in critically ill patients leads to **increased morbidity and mortality**.
2. In critically ill patients receiving **mechanical ventilation**, initiating **enteral nutrition within the first 24 to 48 hours** may be associated with **lower infection risk**, **less time on the ventilator**, **decreased ICU and hospital lengths of stay**, and **decreased hospital mortality**.
3. Benefits of **early enteral nutrition** extend beyond prevention of malnutrition and include **reduced inflammatory response in the gut and lung**, **reduced gut permeability**, **preserved gastrointestinal motility**, and **preservation of commensal gastrointestinal flora**.
4. **Simple gastric feeding** is adequate in most critically ill patients.
5. **Postpyloric feeding** is reserved for patients who **cannot tolerate gastric feeding** or are at **high risk for aspiration**.
6. **Metoclopramide**, a prokinetic agent, may improve enteral nutrition tolerance but **does not improve patient outcomes** and may cause **diarrhea** or **QT interval prolongation**.
7. Guidelines recommend **against routine measurement of gastric residual volume (GRV)** because GRV does **not correlate** with pneumonia, reflux, or aspiration and may cause unnecessary interruptions in enteral nutrition delivery.
8. **Parenteral nutrition** should not be started immediately in patients tolerating enteral feeds. It should only be considered if patients receive **<60% of energy and protein needs** via enteral nutrition for **7 to 10 days**.
9. **Early parenteral nutrition** should be considered in **severely malnourished** patients and those at **high risk of malnutrition** who cannot receive enteral nutrition.

### üìò Related Text Derivations

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. All patients in the **ICU** should undergo a **nutritional evaluation**.
2. **Enteral nutrition** is preferred unless a **contraindication** exists (e.g., **gastrointestinal perforation** or **hemorrhage**).
3. **Early enteral nutrition** is associated with decreased **mortality** and **infections** and should be initiated within **24 to 48 hours of admission** whenever possible.
4. Critically ill patients unable to maintain volitional intake should be fed using a **gastric or postpyloric tube**.
5. **Postpyloric feeding** is recommended for patients at **high risk of aspiration**, but delays in tube placement should not delay enteral nutrition initiation.
6. **Routine gastric residual volume (GRV) measurement** is not recommended because it delays achievement of feeding goals, increases enteral tube clogging risk, and may increase aspiration risk.
7. In patients receiving enteral nutrition, **supplemental parenteral nutrition** should be considered only after **7 to 10 days** of receiving **<60% of energy and protein requirements** by enteral feeding.
8. Adding **parenteral nutrition** earlier than this may cause **harm**.
9. In patients unable to receive enteral feeding and with **adequate baseline nutrition**, **parenteral nutrition** should be initiated after **7 to 10 days**.
10. In patients who are **severely malnourished** or at **high risk for malnutrition** and cannot receive enteral feeding, **parenteral nutrition** should be initiated as soon as possible.
