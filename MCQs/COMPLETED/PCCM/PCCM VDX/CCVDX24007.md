#### âœ… True Statements
1. **Atelectasis** is a **common postoperative complication**.  
2. **Pain** or **impaired respiratory mechanics** after **thoracic or abdominal surgeries** can cause **shallow breathing** and **reduced coughing**, leading to atelectasis.  
3. Patients with **atelectasis** may be **asymptomatic** or present with **labored breathing**, **diminished breath sounds at the lung bases**, **egophony**, **rhonchi**, and **consolidation on imaging**.  
4. Management of **atelectasis** includes **pain control**, **early mobility**, and **secretion management**.  
5. **Respiratory physiotherapy** (including inspiratory muscle training, deep breathing exercise, mobility programs, or percussion and vibration therapy) helps **prevent and treat atelectasis**.  
6. **Bronchoscopy** for routine **airway mucous clearance** offers **no clear benefit** compared with chest physiotherapy and should only be considered if **24 hours of respiratory physiotherapy fails** to improve atelectasis.  
7. In cases with **minimal secretions**, **positive-pressure airway therapy** (e.g., positive expiratory pressure devices) may be used to **recruit collapsed alveoli**.  
8. There is **no evidence** supporting the use of **mucolytics** such as **N-acetylcysteine** for the treatment of atelectasis.

#### ğŸ’¬ Extra
1. Postoperative atelectasis is especially frequent after thoracic and abdominal surgeries.  
2. Pain and impaired mechanics discourage deep breathing and coughing, which normally clear secretions.  
3. Clinical and imaging findings may mimic pneumonia, but reflect collapse rather than infection.  
4. Pain relief, mobilization, and secretion clearance target the underlying contributors to collapse.  
5. Physiotherapy methods directly improve lung expansion and secretion clearance.  
6. Bronchoscopy is reserved for refractory cases, not as a first-line intervention.  
7. Positive expiratory pressure devices help open collapsed alveoli when secretions are not obstructive.  
8. N-acetylcysteine and similar mucolytics are not supported for this indication.
