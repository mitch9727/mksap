### ü´Å Pulmonary Medicine: Pneumothorax Diagnosis with Point-of-Care Ultrasonography

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. The **lung point sign** on ultrasonography is consistent with **pneumothorax**.  
2. The **lung point sign** represents the **transition point** between normal pleura with lung sliding and pneumothorax with absent sliding.  
3. On one side of the **lung point**, there is **pleural sliding**; on the other side, the **pleural line is still with absent sliding**.  
4. The **lung point sign** has **almost 100% specificity** but only **66% sensitivity** for diagnosing pneumothorax.  
5. On ultrasonography, **pleural effusion** appears as a **stable anechoic region** within the chest cavity.  
6. On ultrasonography, **pneumonia** appears as an **area of consolidation with B-lines** and may cause **loss of lung sliding** but does not produce a lung point.  
7. On ultrasonography, **pulmonary fibrosis** produces a **thickened, irregular pleural line** and a **diffuse B-line pattern**.  
8. In pneumothorax, the pleural line may appear **hyperechoic (white) but lacks continuous sliding** across the entire field.

#### üí¨ Extra
1. The **lung point** is the only ultrasonographic finding considered pathognomonic for pneumothorax.  
6. Pneumonia-related **loss of sliding** results from inflammation and adhesions, not free air.

### üìò Related Text Derivations

#### ‚úÖ True Statements ‚Äî Evaluation of Pneumothorax
1. **Primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP)** occurs **without underlying lung disease**, whereas **secondary spontaneous pneumothorax (SSP)** occurs **with underlying lung disease such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)**.
2. **Recurrence risk** after **primary spontaneous pneumothorax** is **23% to 50% over 1 to 5 years**.
3. **Recurrence risk** after **secondary spontaneous pneumothorax** is **greater than 50% over 1 to 3 years**.
4. Symptoms of **pneumothorax** include **sudden dyspnea** and **sharp pleuritic chest pain**, and symptoms are **typically more severe in secondary spontaneous pneumothorax**.
5. Physical examination in **pneumothorax** commonly shows **reduced lung expansion**, **hyperresonance to percussion**, and **diminished breath sounds** on the affected side.
6. **Tension pneumothorax** should be **suspected** when significant **cardiorespiratory distress** is present, including **worsening dyspnea**, **hypotension**, **absent breath sounds** on one side, **tracheal deviation**, and **distended neck veins**.
7. **Chest radiography** is an **appropriate initial test** for suspected pneumothorax because it can **confirm the diagnosis** and **determine pneumothorax size**.
8. On chest radiography, a **lung margin greater than 2 cm from the chest wall at the level of the hilum** defines a **large pneumothorax**.
9. **Computed tomography (CT)** is the **most sensitive** imaging modality for **small pneumothoraces**.
10. **Computed tomography (CT)** is **particularly useful** for evaluating pneumothorax in **bullous emphysema**.
11. **Thoracic ultrasonography** can aid diagnosis of pneumothorax; the **presence of lung sliding** at a scanned point indicates **no pneumothorax at that location**.
12. On thoracic ultrasonography, the **lung point** **confirms the edge** of a pneumothorax.
13. **Thoracic ultrasonography** is **indicated in unstable patients** with concern for **tension physiology**.
14. On thoracic ultrasonography, **lung sliding** represents **visceral pleura moving against parietal pleura with respiration**.
15. **Absent lung sliding** can occur **without pneumothorax** in the presence of **pulmonary blebs, inflammation, scarring, adhesions,** or **apnea**.
16. In **large pneumothorax**, a **lung point may not be identifiable** on ultrasonography.

#### üí¨ Extra ‚Äî Evaluation of Pneumothorax
8. The **>2 cm** threshold is measured **at the level of the hilum** on the chest radiograph.
11. **Lung sliding** excludes pneumothorax **only at the specific location scanned**.
12. Identification of a **lung point** is **specific** for pneumothorax and provides an estimate of **pneumothorax size**.
13. Point‚Äëof‚Äëcare ultrasound is useful when immediate bedside assessment is needed for **hemodynamically unstable** patients.
