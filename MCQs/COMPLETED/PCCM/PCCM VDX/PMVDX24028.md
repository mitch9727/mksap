### ü´Å Pulmonology: Pleural Effusion Recognition on CT

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Pleural effusions** appear on **chest computed tomography (CT) scans** as **smooth opacifications within the pleural space**.
2. **Pleural effusions** in a **supine patient** typically layer in the **most dependent spaces**.
3. **Bilateral small pleural effusions** can be identified on CT by their symmetric appearance in the pleural spaces.
4. **Mesothelioma** on chest imaging typically presents with a **unilateral pleural abnormality** and a **large unilateral pleural effusion**, in contrast to the bilateral effusions seen here.

#### ‚úÖ True Statements ‚Äî Diagnostic Imaging in the Evaluation of Pleural Effusion
1. **Chest radiography** should be the **first test** performed to evaluate a possible **pleural effusion**.
2. As little as **200 mL of pleural fluid** can be detected on a **posterior‚Äìanterior chest radiograph**, and **50 mL** can be detected on a **lateral chest radiograph**.
3. **Thoracic point-of-care ultrasonography** is useful for identifying **small pleural effusions**, especially in **semirecumbent or supine patients**, such as those who are **critically ill**.
4. **Thoracic ultrasonography** allows estimation of **pleural fluid volume** and determination of whether the effusion is **free-flowing or loculated**.
5. **Ultrasound guidance** for pleural procedures such as **thoracentesis** decreases the **rate of complications**.
6. **Chest CT with contrast** can detect **small pleural effusions**.
7. **Chest CT with contrast** can assess **coexisting intrathoracic abnormalities**, including **pulmonary and pleural masses**.
8. On CT imaging, **empyema** is suggested by **enhancement of the pleura around pleural fluid** producing a **lenticular-shaped opacity**.

#### ‚úÖ True Statements ‚Äî Chest Radiographs Showing Various Pleural Effusions
1. On chest radiograph, a **moderate pleural effusion** may appear as **layering fluid over the lower hemithorax**.
2. A **large pleural effusion** can produce the **meniscus sign**, seen as a **rim of fluid ascending the lateral chest wall**.
3. A **loculated pleural effusion** may present as a **lateral chest wall opacity** with **septations** and **separate compartments**.
4. **Bilateral multiloculated pleural effusions** can occur, with **multiple compartmentalized fluid collections** visible on chest radiographs.

#### ‚úÖ True Statements ‚Äî Ultrasound Showing Moderate Right-Sided Pleural Effusion
1. On thoracic ultrasound, a **pleural effusion** appears as an **anechoic or hypoechoic space** between the **lung and diaphragm**.

#### ‚úÖ True Statements ‚Äî Simple and Complicated Pleural Effusions on Ultrasound
1. A **simple pleural effusion** on ultrasound appears as a **hypoechoic collection**, which may be either **transudative or exudative**.
2. A **complicated pleural effusion** on ultrasound appears as a **multiseptated (loculated) fluid collection**, which is always **exudative**.

#### ‚úÖ True Statements ‚Äî Fluid Pocket and Pleural Thickening on CT Scan in Empyema
1. On CT imaging, an **empyema** may show a **pleural fluid pocket** with **enhancing pleura** producing a **lenticular-shaped opacity**.
2. **Pleural thickening** may also be present on CT imaging in **empyema**.
