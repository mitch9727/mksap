1. **Surgical procedures** are not first-line therapy for **obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)** but can be effective in carefully selected patients.  
2. **Maxillomandibular advancement surgery** has been shown to significantly improve the **apnea-hypopnea index (AHI)**, even in severe OSA.  
3. A **higher body mass index (BMI)** reduces the efficacy of surgical treatments for OSA.  
4. Patients with OSA and a **BMI less than 40** who are unwilling or unable to use **positive airway pressure (PAP) therapy** should be considered for sleep surgery, such as maxillomandibular advancement.

8. **Oral appliances** are appropriate for **mild to moderate OSA (AHI <30 events/hour)** but are not clearly effective for severe OSA.  
9. **Key Point:** Surgical treatments for obstructive sleep apnea can be considered for patients who do not benefit from or tolerate airway pressure therapy.
