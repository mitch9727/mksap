1. **Cryptogenic organizing pneumonia (COP)** typically presents subacutely with **cough, fever, and malaise** developing over weeks to months.
2. **Chest radiographs** in cryptogenic organizing pneumonia commonly show **patchy opacities that mimic community-acquired pneumonia (CAP)**.
3. Lack of response to **antibiotics** and persistence of symptoms should raise suspicion for **cryptogenic organizing pneumonia**.
4. **High-resolution chest CT (HRCT)** in cryptogenic organizing pneumonia can show findings ranging from **ground-glass opacities** to **alveolar consolidation** that resemble infectious pneumonia.
5. The diagnosis of **cryptogenic organizing pneumonia** requires exclusion of other infectious and noninfectious causes and demonstration of **consistent radiographic changes**.
6. **Lung biopsy** may not be necessary in cryptogenic organizing pneumonia if clinical and radiographic findings are typical, but when performed, it shows **granulation tissue forming intraluminal fibroblastic plugs** in **alveolar sacs, alveolar ducts, and bronchiolar lumen**.
7. The clinical presentation of **cryptogenic organizing pneumonia** is often mistaken for **community-acquired pneumonia**.

#### ‚úÖ True Statements ‚Äî Cryptogenic Organizing Pneumonia
1. **Organizing pneumonia** is defined histopathologically by **patchy proliferation of granulation tissue** in **terminal bronchioles, alveolar ducts, and alveolar spaces**, with associated surrounding inflammation.
2. The organizing pneumonia pattern is nonspecific and can follow or be associated with **lung injury from acute infection, radiation, drug-induced pneumonitis, or autoimmune disease**.
3. When no specific cause is identified, the condition is termed **cryptogenic organizing pneumonia (COP)**.
4. Patients with **cryptogenic organizing pneumonia** typically present subacutely with **cough, dyspnea, fever, and malaise**.
5. **Chest radiographs** in cryptogenic organizing pneumonia often show **patchy opacities mimicking pneumonia**, leading to frequent initial misdiagnosis.
6. **Nonresolving symptoms** and **failure to improve with antibiotics** should raise suspicion for **cryptogenic organizing pneumonia**.
7. **High-resolution chest CT (HRCT)** findings in cryptogenic organizing pneumonia range from **ground-glass opacities** to **alveolar consolidation**.
8. **Lung biopsy** is not always required for diagnosis if clinical and HRCT findings are consistent, but it can help in atypical cases.
9. **Glucocorticoid therapy** is effective in cryptogenic organizing pneumonia, with most patients showing a good response.
10. **Relapses** of cryptogenic organizing pneumonia are common during glucocorticoid tapering, and some patients may need **glucocorticoid-sparing therapies**.
