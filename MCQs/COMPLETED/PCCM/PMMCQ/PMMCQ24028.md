### ü´Å Pulmonary Medicine: Secondary Spontaneous Pneumothorax Treatment

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Spontaneous pneumothorax** is classified as a **primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP)** in the absence of underlying lung disease or as a **secondary spontaneous pneumothorax (SSP)** when it occurs in the setting of underlying lung disease, such as **chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)**.
2. In **secondary spontaneous pneumothorax (SSP)**, the recurrence rate of an ipsilateral pneumothorax is as high as **39%**.
3. In patients with **underlying lung disease** who have a **first episode of secondary spontaneous pneumothorax**, **pleurodesis** is indicated to prevent recurrence.
4. **Chemical pleurodesis** involves instillation of a **sclerosing agent**, such as **talc**, into the pleural space.
5. **Mechanical pleurodesis** involves **mechanical abrasion** of the pleural membranes through **thoracoscopy**.
6. In patients with **severe COPD** and **secondary spontaneous pneumothorax**, **chemical pleurodesis** is preferred because such patients are **poor surgical candidates**.
7. An **autologous blood patch pleurodesis** involves instilling a sample of the patient's blood into the pleural space through a **thoracostomy tube**, followed by hanging the chest drain above the patient‚Äôs head for **1‚Äì2 hours** to prevent drainage of blood or thrombus through the tube.
8. **Autologous blood patch pleurodesis** is typically used for a **persistent air leak** after pneumothorax, not after a first episode without persistent leak.
9. **Endobronchial valves**, which allow air to exit but not enter the damaged area of the lung, are a **second-line therapy** for pneumothorax with **persistent air leak**.
10. Removal of a **thoracostomy tube alone** is appropriate in a patient with **primary spontaneous pneumothorax** after a first episode, but it is **inappropriate** in **secondary spontaneous pneumothorax** due to high recurrence risk and severe underlying disease.

#### ‚úÖ True Statements (from Table: Management of Pneumothorax Based on Size and Clinical Symptoms)
1. Patients with **minimal symptoms** and **clinical stability**, regardless of pneumothorax size, should be managed with **hospitalization for observation** and **supplemental oxygen**.  
2. **Outpatient management** of **primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP)** may be considered if there is **good access to medical care**.  
3. For a pneumothorax **greater than 2 cm on chest radiograph** or with **significant symptoms** (such as **dyspnea** or **chest pain**), management options include **needle aspiration**, **insertion of an ambulatory device**, or **insertion of a small-bore (<14 Fr [4.7 mm]) thoracostomy tube** with connection to a **high-volume, low-pressure suction system**.  
4. Patients with **cardiovascular compromise** (such as **hypotension** or **increasing breathlessness**) should undergo **emergent needle decompression** followed by **thoracostomy tube insertion**.  
5. If there is a **persistent air leak lasting longer than 48 hours**, referral to an **interventional pulmonologist** or **thoracic surgeon** is recommended.  

#### üí¨ Extra
1. Supplemental oxygen increases the gradient for nitrogen absorption, accelerating resolution of pleural air.  
3. The threshold of **>2 cm** is measured between the **lung and chest wall** at the **level of the hilum**.
