### ü´Å Pulmonary Medicine: Airway Obstruction and Flow-Volume Loops

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Flow-volume loops** can localize anatomic sites of airway obstruction.  
2. A **flattened expiratory flow limb** on spirometry suggests an **intrathoracic airway obstruction**.  
3. A **flattened inspiratory flow limb** on spirometry suggests an **extrathoracic airway obstruction**.  
4. A **fixed airway obstruction** results in **flattening of both inspiratory and expiratory flow limbs**.  
5. **Chest computed tomography (CT)** is the most appropriate test to confirm an **intrathoracic airway obstruction** because it allows direct visualization of the airways.  
6. Causes of **variable extrathoracic obstruction** include **tracheomalacia**, **tumors**, and **vocal fold paralysis**.  
7. Causes of **variable intrathoracic obstruction** include **intrathoracic tumors** and **tracheomalacia**.  
8. **Tracheal stenosis** or **tumor** usually causes **fixed obstruction**.  
9. Patients with a **focal wheeze** should undergo **chest CT** to rule out an **endobronchial lesion**.  

#### üí¨ Extra
1. Flow-volume loops graphically represent **maximally forced inspiratory and expiratory flow (Y-axis)** versus **volume (X-axis)**.  
2. Fractional exhaled nitric oxide testing assesses **airway inflammation** in **asthma** and **nonasthmatic eosinophilic bronchitis**, but it is not indicated when obstruction is suspected.  
3. Laryngoscopy is the test of choice for **inducible laryngeal obstruction (vocal cord dysfunction)**, which causes **flattening of the inspiratory limb** (extrathoracic pattern).
