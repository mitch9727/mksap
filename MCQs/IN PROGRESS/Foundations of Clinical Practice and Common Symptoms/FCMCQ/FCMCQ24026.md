### ü©∫ Foundations of Clinical Practice: Motivational Interviewing (Behavior Change Counseling)

#### ‚úÖ True Statements
1. **Motivational interviewing** is predicated on the belief that individuals have within themselves the means to change their behavior.
2. In **motivational interviewing**, the clinician's role is to **partner** with the patient, **accept** and have **compassion** for their situation, and **guide** conversations to help them **evoke their own reasons for change** rather than imposing the clinician's reasons.
3. **Motivational interviewing** uses the **OARS mnemonic**‚Äî**Open-ended questions**, **Affirmations**, **Reflections**, and **Summaries**‚Äîto guide rather than direct behavior-change conversations.
4. An **initial collaborative and supportive stance** can increase a patient's willingness to accept recommendations when the patient is already aware of their risk; repeating known risk information is less productive.
5. Providing **directives** (for example, recommending a specific activity) **without first eliciting** what the patient has already tried and what they can fit into their day is **less consistent** with motivational interviewing.
6. **Asking open-ended questions** about what the patient thinks about their situation and what they feel they **can and want to do** is **more consistent** with motivational interviewing than immediately recommending pharmacologic therapy.
7. **Pharmacologic therapy** may **delay or prevent the onset of diabetes** in at-risk individuals, but motivational interviewing emphasizes **eliciting the patient‚Äôs perspective before making recommendations**.

#### üí¨ Extra
5. Exploring **prior attempts** and **daily constraints** helps identify behavior-change options that are feasible for the patient.
6. After eliciting responses, recommendations should be **concordant with the patient‚Äôs desires and abilities**.

#### ‚úÖ True Statements ‚Äî Motivational Interviewing
1. **Motivational interviewing** is a **versatile**, **patient-centered**, and **evidence-based** behavioral counseling model.
2. **Motivational interviewing** has been shown to **reduce alcohol consumption**, **binge drinking**, and **substance use** and to **increase medication adherence and physical activity**.
3. **Motivational interviewing** presupposes that patients have within themselves **both the reasons and the answers** for change.
4. The **PACE approach** in motivational interviewing comprises **Partnering and collaborating** with the patient, **Accepting** and having **Compassion** for the patient‚Äôs situation, and **Evoking** the patient‚Äôs own reasons to change their behavior instead of imposing the provider‚Äôs reasons.
5. Core skills in motivational interviewing are summarized by the **OARS mnemonic**: **Open-ended questions**, **Affirmations**, **Reflections**, and **Summaries**.
6. The **process of motivational interviewing** includes **eliciting the patient‚Äôs perspective**, **focusing on the desired behavior**, **evoking the patient‚Äôs reasons for change without judgment**, and **planning next steps for change**.
7. **Motivational interviewing** is **most effective** when a patient‚Äôs **ambivalence is high** or when the **importance**, **desire**, or **confidence** to change is **low**.

---
8. In **motivational interviewing**, **open-ended questions** are used to elicit and explore the individual's experiences, perspectives, and ideas.
9. In **motivational interviewing**, **affirmations** emphasize strengths, efforts, and past successes to build hope and confidence in the individual's ability to change.
10. In **motivational interviewing**, **reflections** may be simple (repeating) or complex (deeper guesses) and are based on careful listening to understand the individual's perspective.
11. In **motivational interviewing**, **summaries** ensure mutual understanding and reinforce key points expressed by the individual, including highlighting ambivalence and weighing pros and cons.
12. During the **Engage** step of motivational interviewing, the clinician seeks a genuine understanding of behavior in a nonjudgmental way.
13. During **Engage**, the clinician reflects back what the individual says‚Äîusing slightly different wording if needed‚Äîto facilitate understanding and establish rapport.
14. During **Engage**, the clinician avoids making assumptions about the individual's motivation.
15. During the **Focus** step, the clinician listens for **change talk** (positive reasons for change) versus **sustain talk** (reasons to maintain the status quo).
16. During **Focus**, the clinician acknowledges sustain talk and gently encourages elaboration during change talk.
17. When **focusing**, the clinician may use elaboration prompts, affirmations, and reflections as micro-skills.
18. When **reflecting** during the Focus step, the clinician makes a guess in the form of a statement.
19. During the **Evoke** step, the clinician helps the individual formulate and verbalize new goals, hopes, reasons to change, and an initial step.
20. If the individual is unable to express goals, hopes, or reasons to change during **Evoke**, the clinician may offer suggestions based on what others have done in similar situations.
21. During the **Plan** step, the clinician discusses specific goals, resources to achieve them, and parameters for monitoring progress and evaluating success.
22. During **Plan**, the clinician identifies barriers to success and creates strategies in advance to address these barriers.
