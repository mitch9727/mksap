1. All **preoperative laboratory testing** should be tailored to the **nature of the surgery**, **known comorbid conditions**, and **findings from the history or physical examination**.
2. **Routine preoperative laboratory panels** are not recommended because they **rarely guide management**.
3. **Hemoglobin testing** is indicated for **orthopedic procedures** associated with **significant blood loss** to screen for **anemia**.
4. If **anemia** is identified before **elective surgery**, the operation should be **delayed** for evaluation and correction of the anemia to improve surgical outcomes.
5. Use of **hydrochlorothiazide** and **lisinopril** justifies **electrolyte** and **creatinine** testing before surgery because these medications may alter those levels.
6. The most useful screening tool for **bleeding complications of surgery** is the **bleeding history**, not **routine coagulation testing**.
7. **Coagulation testing** is not needed before **elective orthopedic surgery** in patients without a history of abnormal bleeding, anticoagulant use, or coagulopathy.
8. **Liver chemistry testing** is not indicated before surgery in patients without a history of liver disease or recent abnormal liver test results.
9. If the patient is **clinically stable** and there are **no medication changes**, tests do not need to be repeated if they were performed within **4 months** of the procedure.
10. **Urinalysis** to screen for **asymptomatic bacteriuria** is not indicated before **elective orthopedic surgery** in the absence of **urinary symptoms**.
