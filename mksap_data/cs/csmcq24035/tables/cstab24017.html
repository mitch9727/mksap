ACL Integrity

Anterior drawer

The patient is supine with the hip flexed to 45 degrees and knee flexed to 90 degrees. The examiner sits on the dorsum of the foot and places their hands on the proximal calf and then pulls anteriorly while assessing movement of tibia relative to femur. ( Video: Anterior Drawer Test)

Positive result: Increased laxity with lack of a firm end point suggests an ACL tear.

Positive likelihood ratio: 3.8 a

Negative likelihood ratio: 0.3 a

Lachman

The patient is supine with the leg in slight external rotation and the knee flexed 20-30 degrees at the examiner's side. The examiner stabilizes the femur with one hand and grasps the proximal calf with the other. The examiner pulls the calf while assessing movement of the tibia relative to the femur. (Video: Lachman Test)

Positive result: Increased laxity with lack of a firm end point suggests an ACL tear.

Positive likelihood ratio: 42 b

Negative likelihood ratio: 0.1 b

PCL Integrity

Posterior drawer

The patient is supine with the hip flexed to 45 degrees and the knee flexed to 90 degrees. The examiner sits on the dorsum of the foot and places their hands on the proximal calf and then pushes posteriorly while assessing movement of the tibia relative to femur.

Positive result: Increased laxity with lack of a firm end point suggests a PCL tear.

Positive likelihood ratio: 50.1 c

Negative likelihood ratio: 0.1 c

MCL Integrity

Valgus stress

The patient is supine with the knee flexed to 30 degrees and the leg slightly abducted. The examiner places one hand on the lateral knee and the other hand on the medial distal tibia and applies valgus (toward the midline) force. The test can also be done with an extended knee. (Video: Valgus Stress Test)

Positive result: Increased laxity and pain suggest an MCL tear.

Positive likelihood ratio: 7.7 d

Negative likelihood ratio: 0.2 d

LCL Integrity

Varus stress

The patient is supine with the knee flexed to 30 degrees and the leg slightly abducted. The examiner places one hand on the medial knee and the other hand on the lateral distal tibia and applies varus (away from the midline) force. The test can also be done with an extended knee.

Positive result: Increased laxity and pain suggest an LCL tear.

Positive likelihood ratio: 16.2 d

Meniscal Integrity

Thessaly

The examiner holds the patient's outstretched hands while the patient stands on one leg with the knee flexed to 5 degrees and the other knee flexed to 90 degrees with the foot off the floor. The patient rotates their body internally and externally three times. The test should be repeated with the knee flexed to 20 degrees. The test should always be performed on the uninvolved knee first. (Video: Thessaly Test)

Positive result: Medial or lateral joint line pain suggests a meniscal tear.

Positive likelihood ratio: 1.4 e

Negative likelihood ratio: 0.7 e

Medial-lateral grind

With the patient supine, the examiner places the calf in one hand and the thumb and index finger of the opposite hand over joint line and applies varus and valgus stress to the tibia during extension and flexion.

Positive result: A grinding sensation that is palpable over the joint line suggests meniscal injury.

Positive likelihood ratio: 4.8 b

Negative likelihood ratio: 0.4 b

McMurray

With the patient supine, the examiner fully flexes the knee and rotates the tibia externally. The knee is then extended with the hand over the medial joint line. The maneuver is then repeated with the tibia internally rotated and the hand over the lateral joint line.

Positive result: Snapping is detected over the joint line with extension on the knee.

Positive likelihood ratio: 1.3 b

Negative likelihood ratio: 0.8 b

Iliotibial Band Syndrome

Noble f

With the patient supine, the examiner repeatedly flexes and extends the knee with examiner's thumb placed on the lateral femoral epicondyle. (Video: Noble Test)

Positive result: Iliotibial band syndrome is suggested if this maneuver reproduces the patient's pain.
