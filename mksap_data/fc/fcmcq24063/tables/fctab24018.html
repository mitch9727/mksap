Example

Closed-loop communication/check-backs

1. Sender initiates message.

2. Receiver accepts message and provides feedback.

3. Sender double-checks to ensure that message was received correctly.

Used to ensure that information conveyed by the sender is understood by the receiver as intended

Physician: “Give 5 mg metoprolol IV push.”

Nurse: “5 mg metoprolol IV push.”

Physician: “Yes, that's correct.”

SBAR

S ituation: What is going on with the patient?

B ackground: What is the clinical background or context?

A ssessment: What are the objective/subjective findings? What do you think the problem is?

R ecommendation/Request: What do you think needs to be done?

Used for communicating critical information about a patient's condition that requires attention and action

S: “I am calling about Mrs. ___ with concerns about new hypoxemia.”

B: “She is postop day 2 after total knee replacement and has not had an O 2 requirement. After dinner, the O 2 sat was 87% on room air. Patient is alert with some respiratory distress and wheezes on examination. Other vital signs were normal.”

A: “I am worried about aspiration pneumonia.”

R: “I think the patient needs evaluation and possible chest x-ray.”

Call-outs

Communicating important or critical information verbally, which informs all team members simultaneously

Emergent settings (e.g., code blue, rapid response team)

Code leader: “Airway status?”

Resident: “Airway clear.”

Code leader: “Breath sounds?”

Resident: “Breath sounds decreased on right.”

Code leader: “Blood pressure?”

Nurse: “BP is 96/62.”

I-PASS

I llness severity: stability level of patient

P atient summary: events leading to admission, hospital course, assessment, and plan

A ction list: to-do list for patient, timeline, and ownership

S ituation awareness and contingency planning: plan for what might happen

S ynthesis: recipient of patient information summarizes what was heard, asks questions, and restates action items

Used during handoffs between clinicians in hospital

Designed to enhance information exchange during transitions of care

I: “Mr. ___ is the sickest patient on our service and will need careful monitoring.”

P: “He is a 57-year-old man with diabetes and cellulitis of the right leg. He was admitted with hypotension, fever, and concerns for fasciitis. He has stabilized after 2 liters of normal saline and vancomycin and piperacillin/tazobactam but continues to have fevers and BPs in the 90s/50s. Leg CT is pending.”

A: “Vancomycin level needs to be followed up at 8 pm and vital signs and volume status evaluated every 2 hours.”

S: “If SBP is <80 mm Hg and there's no volume overload, give 500 mL NS IV bolus. If vancomycin level is <15, discuss with pharmacy dose changes. Follow up on leg CT.”

S: “Okay, so Mr. ___ has severe cellulitis with hypotension and is on broad-spectrum antibiotics. He will need vital signs and volume exam every 2 hours and may require vancomycin dose adjustment and additional BP support with fluids. Follow-up on leg CT. Did I miss anything?”

Team/family meeting

Summarizes patient status and coordinates next steps of care with family members

Hospitalist, patient, family, representatives from key consulting disciplines, social work, and care coordination are in attendance

Occurs during hospitalization

Often held when there is a change in clinical course, new events, or change in goals of care
