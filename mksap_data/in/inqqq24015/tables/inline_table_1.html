Treatment Strategies for Urinary Incontinence

Incontinence Type

Pharmacologic Therapy

Other Therapies

Stress

Pelvic floor muscle training (Kegel exercises) with or without biofeedback

Instruct patient to contract the pelvic floor as if attempting to avoid urination and sustain the contraction for 10 seconds. Contractions should be performed in three or four sets of 10 each day.

Topical vaginal estrogen (postmenopausal women)

Pessaries, injectable bulking agents, sling cystourethropexy

Urge (overactive bladder with incontinence)

Bladder training/timed voiding

Instruct patient to schedule voiding attempts at intervals shorter than the usual time between incontinence episodes, regardless of the urge to void, gradually increasing the time between voids.

If urgency occurs before the designated voiding time, instruct patients to use pelvic floor muscle contraction until the urge passes and then proceed with voiding directly afterward.

Anticholinergic/antimuscarinics (oxybutynin, darifenacin, solifenacin, tolterodine, fesoterodine, trospium)

β-Adrenergics (mirabegron, vibegron)

Topical vaginal estrogen (postmenopausal women)

Spinal neuromodulators, botulinum toxin injections

Overflow

Double voiding

Instruct patient to remain relaxed at the toilet for a minute after voiding and then attempt to void again.

Triggered voiding

Instruct patient to stimulate voiding and encourage complete emptying by leaning forward, squeezing the pelvic floor at the end of urination to promote emptying; also massaging the umbilicus, suprapubic area, and tapping the bladder.

α-Blockers (alfuzosin, doxazosin, prazosin, terazosin, silodosin, tamsulosin)

5α-Reductase inhibitors (dutasteride and finasteride) for BPH-related symptoms

Transurethral prostatectomy can be considered for BPH-related symptoms

Scheduled intermittent catheterization

Functional

Caregiver-prompted timed voiding

No recommended pharmacologic therapies

None routinely recommended
